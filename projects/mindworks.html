Center for Decision Research

We researched, analyzed, and refined the visitor experience for CDR, a public behavorial science lab and discovery center in downtown Chicago.

September 2020

My Role: UX Design Consultant, Researcher, Analysis, Synthesis, Ideation

Research Methods: Interviews, Survey, Literature Review, Competitive Analysis

Team: Gurpreet Kaur, Lynn Baus, Irandy Perez, Lina Moon

Executive Summary

We partnered with the University of Chicago’s Center for Decision Research (CDR) with the goal to increase both the number and diversity of visitors to their new discovery center in downtown Chicago. The center will feature interactive exhibits to help inform the general public about the importance of behavioral science and how they can leverage behavioral science to make better life decisions. In addition, CDR is a research hub, which aims to encourage visitors to participate in scientific research studies.

We conducted primary and secondary research where we learned what motivates individuals to visit museums, whether they explore alone or in a group, and possible deterrents to their future visits. As a result we identified patterns in the form of archetypes, which led us to solutions that increase both the number and diversity of visitors to CDR.

Goals & Questions

Our primary goal was to gain a better understanding of the intended audience for CDR. Specifically we aimed to:

  • Understand the CDR team's current goals, plans and research regarding the intended audience

  • Understand what drives people to visit learner-focused spaces such as museums

  • Discover the factors that lead visitors to believe their experience was valuable

From these goals we drafted a series of research questions to guide our process:

  • How might we encourage the general public to visit the CDR experience?

  • What are the diverse and salient motivations, goals and attitudes that determine how and why members of the public would be compelled to visit the CDR experience?

  • How might we inspire exhibit visitors and study participants to continue to feel a connection with the CDR experience after their visit has been completed?

Research Methods

Stakeholder & Expert Interviews

To kick off the project, we sought to understand previous research, plans and stakeholders’ goals for CDR. In order to do so, we conducted a virtual workshop with CDR staff. We also conducted an expert interview with a museum exhibit designer (AJ Goehle, CEO of Luci Creative) in order to understand how the industry approaches audience research and definition.

Audience Interviews

We conducted virtual interviews to understand what drives people to visit exhibits similar to CDR and to validate previous marketing research. We recruited 15 participants through convenience sampling. We aimed to reach participants who were not primarily undergraduate or graduate college students. During the interviews, we asked them what caused them to visit the venue, what they did while they were there, and whether they shared their experiences post-visit. After the interviews, we synthesized salient points from our transcripts and notes into an affinity diagram to develop major themes.

Audience Survey

We conducted a quantitative survey to further prioritize and validate our findings from qualitative research. The survey featured 14 questions, and a total of 43 participants completed the survey.

Literature Review

To gain insight into how CDR may effectively define and activate audiences, we performed a literature review of nine sources covering the areas of museum studies, tourism, civic activation methods, and retail customer engagement. We learned that a critical predictor of the likelihood of making a visit and post-visit satisfaction is how closely the exhibit experience matches one of five temporary identity-based motivations: Explorer, Experience Seeker, Professional/Hobbyist, Facilitator, and Recharger. Because CDR is positioned in a heavily touristed location, we also reviewed research on the motivations behind a tourist’s choice of destination. While many tourist motivations categories overlapped with the museum visitors categories, an additional category emerged: Entertainment Seeker, which represents a non-learner based motivation.

Key Insights

Primary Insights

  • Visitor motivation is more salient than demographic or psychographic formation.

  • Visitors may have different motivations for each visit

  • Visitors' satisfaction depends on how well the experience matched their motivations.

  • Visitors may come as a group but each visitor has their own motivations

  • Visitors want to feel good about accomplishing their goals before and after their visit.

Secondary Insights

  • People perceive science centers as primarily kid-oriented.

  • Downtown workers report visitng after hours, not before or during a work break.

  • People are most deterred by crowds and long waits.

Archetypes

Based on our primary and secondary research, we defined seven archetypes for CDR that capture key visitor motivations.

Primary

  • Explorers are visitors who are generally curious and interested in the topic of the exhibit.They want to learn and expect to find something that captures their interest

  • Experience Seekers are visitors who believe that museums and exhibits are important places to experience. They often want to “check off” visiting a place while traveling.

  • Professionals and hobbyists are visitors who are attracted to the content of an exhibitdue to their profession, hobby, or strong personal connection. They are often looking to gain specific knowledge.

  • Facilitators are visitors who help along the learning and experience of others accompanying them, similar to a mentor or guide.

Secondary

  • Rechargers are visitors who want to relax, reflect, and have a contemplative experience while visiting. They want to take a break from the rest of the world and take refuge.

  • Entertainment Seekers are visitors who are just there to have fun. They want to mix with fellow visitors, find distraction, and be entertained by the content.

  • Tag-Alongs are visitors who are socially motivated. They come to a museum or exhibit with another person or in a group setting. They are primarily there as a companion to the person(s) they are with.
